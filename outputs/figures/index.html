This report covers 381,999 Yelp reviews from 8,203 coffee-chain locations across 13 U.S. states, 2017–2021. The focus is Starbucks — compared against Dunkin' (6,866 reviews) and the broader independent café market to understand where the gaps are and what customers keep writing about. VADER sentiment scoring, rule-based topic tagging, and reviewer segmentation are used throughout.

NB 05

Review Volume Trends

All brands peaked in 2019, then dropped 44% in 2020 when pandemic restrictions cut in-store dining. By 2021, volume had recovered to about 58% of the 2019 peak — and every brand followed roughly the same arc. The dataset covers 508 cities across 13 states. Arizona and Nevada contribute the most reviews; Nevada also has the highest average rating of any state here.

NB 06

Rating Distribution

Starbucks ratings split at the extremes: 38.2% are 5-star, 24.0% are 1-star. There isn't much in the middle. This is a polarized customer base, not one that's broadly disappointed. VADER sentiment scores follow a similar pattern — gradually declining from 2019 onward, a trend that started before the pandemic and kept going after it.

NB 07

Voice of Customer

Reviews are tagged using multi-label keyword matching — a single review can carry more than one topic tag. "The barista was rude and my latte tasted burnt" would be tagged both Service and Food Quality. Topic counts therefore reflect every review that mentions a topic, not just its dominant theme. Service generates the most review volume of any category; Price has the lowest positive rate (23.8%), but on far fewer mentions.

Ambiance is the one topic where Starbucks wins more than it loses: 62.2% positive. Service tells the opposite story. NB 08

Reviewer Segmentation

Reviewers were split into four tiers by lifetime Yelp activity: Casual (under 10 reviews), Regular (10–50), Power (50–200), and Elite (200+). Casual reviewers rate Starbucks at 2.43★ on average, the lowest of any tier. Elite reviewers average 3.60★. First-time or infrequent reviewers appear to write disproportionately after bad experiences, which pulls the casual average down and skews the brand's overall score.

Key Finding

Casual reviewers rate Starbucks 1.17★ lower than Elite ones. On Yelp, first-time reviewers tend to write because something went wrong, not because it went right. That self-selection pulls the casual average down hard.

Summary

Conclusions

Five findings from 11,675 Starbucks reviews, 2017–2021. Each one points somewhere specific.

01

Service is a coin flip

75.6% of Starbucks reviews mention service, more than any other topic. Of those, 44.4% are positive and 46.0% are critical. That's not a gap — it's a toss-up. Staff interaction shapes the overall rating more than anything else in the data, and it's also the thing most likely to go either way depending on the day, the shift, or who's at the register. Training consistency across locations is the fix. Nothing else moves this number.

02

Weekend demand exposes the limits

Ratings drop 0.28 stars on Saturdays and Sundays, when review volume is also at its peak. Wait time already runs 49.4% critical on quieter days. Add more customers and a longer line, and it gets worse. The problems aren't new on weekends — weekends just put them in front of more people. Staffing adjustments and pre-shift prep on high-volume days are the clearest operational lever here.

03

First-time visitors leave the most disappointed

Customers with fewer than 10 Yelp reviews rate Starbucks at 2.43 stars. Those with 200+ give it 3.60. The 1.17-star gap between those groups is larger than the gap between Starbucks and the overall market. Regular café visitors adjust their expectations. People who don't come in often don't, and the experience doesn't do much to course-correct. App onboarding, loyalty entry, how staff treat someone who looks a little lost — that's where the first impression is actually made.

04

Ambiance is the one reliable strength — cleanliness is the threat to it

Ambiance is at 62.2% positive, the only topic in this analysis where praise clearly outnumbers complaints. The physical space holds up even when service and food quality don't. The threat is cleanliness: 47.2% positive but 40.5% critical across 1,609 mentions. A sticky table or a messy restroom doesn't erase good furniture and lighting, but it chips away at it. Visible cleanliness during peak hours is the simplest thing keeping this advantage intact.

05

Price complaints are concentrated and consistent

Price has a 66.9% failure rate across 1,027 mentions — the highest of any topic. Volume is lower than Service or Food Quality, but the signal is less noisy: when customers write about price, nearly two-thirds are unhappy. That's not something operations can change. What it does explain is a chunk of the negative sentiment that has nothing to do with staff or product execution — useful context when reading the overall numbers.
